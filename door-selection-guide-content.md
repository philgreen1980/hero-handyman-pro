## Introduction

Choosing the right doors for your home is about more than just aesthetics—it's about security, energy efficiency, durability, and functionality. Whether you're replacing an old entry door, upgrading interior doors, or adding a new door to your home, this guide will help you make informed decisions.

From material options and styles to hardware and installation considerations, we'll walk you through everything you need to know to select doors that enhance your home's beauty, comfort, and value.

---

## Section 1: Types of Doors

### Entry Doors (Exterior)

**Purpose:** Main entrance to your home, providing security, insulation, and curb appeal.

**Key Considerations:**
- Security and durability
- Weather resistance
- Energy efficiency
- Aesthetic impact on home's exterior

**Common Materials:**
- **Steel:** Most secure and affordable, low maintenance, energy-efficient
- **Fiberglass:** Mimics wood appearance, extremely durable, low maintenance
- **Wood:** Classic beauty, customizable, requires regular maintenance
- **Aluminum/Glass:** Modern look, less secure, best for protected entryways

### Interior Doors

**Purpose:** Separate rooms, provide privacy, enhance interior design.

**Common Types:**
- **Panel doors:** Traditional style with raised or flat panels
- **Flush doors:** Smooth, flat surface for modern or minimalist homes
- **French doors:** Glass panes for light and openness between rooms
- **Barn doors:** Sliding doors for space-saving and rustic charm
- **Pocket doors:** Slide into wall cavity to save space

**Common Materials:**
- **Hollow-core:** Lightweight, affordable, suitable for closets
- **Solid-core:** Heavier, better sound insulation, more durable
- **Solid wood:** Premium option, excellent sound dampening

### Patio Doors

**Purpose:** Connect indoor and outdoor living spaces with easy access and natural light.

**Common Types:**
- **Sliding glass doors:** Space-efficient, wide opening, easy operation
- **French patio doors:** Elegant, full opening, traditional style
- **Bi-fold doors:** Multiple panels that fold, maximum opening

**Key Features:**
- Energy-efficient glass (Low-E coating)
- Multi-point locking systems
- Durable weather stripping

### Storm Doors & Screen Doors

**Purpose:** Protect entry door, improve ventilation, add security layer.

**Benefits:**
- Extends life of entry door
- Allows fresh air while keeping insects out
- Additional insulation in winter
- Extra security with reinforced frames

## Section 3: Door Styles & Designs

### Entry Door Styles

**Traditional:**
- Raised panel designs
- Decorative glass inserts
- Classic hardware (brass, bronze)

**Modern/Contemporary:**

- Wood grain texture
- Wrought iron accents
- Arched or plank designs

### Interior Door Styles

**Panel Doors:**
- 2-panel, 4-panel, 6-panel configurations
- Raised or flat panels
- Versatile for any home style

**Shaker Style:**
- Simple, flat panels with clean lines
- Popular in modern and transitional homes

**Glass Panel Doors:**
- French doors with multiple glass panes
- Single-light doors for offices or dining rooms
- Frosted or textured glass for privacy

**Barn Doors:**
- Sliding on exposed hardware
- Space-saving for small rooms

## Section 4: Hardware & Accessories

### Entry Door Hardware

**Handlesets:**
- **Keyed entry:** Traditional lock and key
- **Smart locks:** Keyless entry with smartphone control
- **Keypad locks:** Code entry, no key needed

**Finishes:**
- **Polished brass:** Traditional, requires polishing
- **Satin nickel:** Modern, hides fingerprints
- **Oil-rubbed bronze:** Rustic, warm tone
- **Matte black:** Contemporary, bold statement

**Additional Features:**
- Deadbolts (single or double cylinder)
- Peepholes or door viewers
- Kick plates for durability
- Door knockers

### Interior Door Hardware

**Knobs vs. Levers:**
- **Knobs:** Traditional, compact
- **Levers:** Easier to operate, ADA-compliant

**Privacy vs. Passage:**
- **Privacy locks:** For bedrooms and bathrooms (can lock from inside)
- **Passage sets:** For hallways and closets (no lock)
- **Dummy knobs:** For closets (non-functional, decorative)

**Hinges:**
- Standard butt hinges (most common)
- Ball-bearing hinges (heavy doors, smooth operation)
- Continuous (piano) hinges (extra support)

## Section 5: Energy Efficiency

### Insulation & Energy Ratings

**R-Value:**
- Measures thermal resistance (higher = better insulation)
- Entry doors: Look for R-5 or higher
- Patio doors: Look for R-3 or higher

**Energy Star Certification:**
- Indicates door meets energy efficiency standards
- Can reduce heating/cooling costs by 10-15%
- May qualify for tax credits or rebates

### Glass Options for Doors

**Single-Pane Glass:**
- Least expensive
- Poor insulation
- Not recommended for entry or patio doors

**Double-Pane (Insulated) Glass:**
- Two layers of glass with air or gas between
- Significantly better insulation
- Standard for modern entry and patio doors

**Low-E Coating:**
- Reflects heat while allowing light through
- Reduces UV damage to furniture
- Improves energy efficiency by 30-50%

**Tempered or Laminated Glass:**
- Safety glass that doesn't shatter into sharp pieces
- Required by code for patio doors and large glass panels

## Section 6: Door Sizing & Fit

### Standard Door Sizes

**Entry Doors:**
- Width: 36" (standard), 32" or 42" (less common)
- Height: 80" (standard), 96" (8-foot ceilings)

**Interior Doors:**
- Width: 24", 28", 30", 32", 36"
- Height: 80" (standard), 96" (tall ceilings)

**Patio Doors:**
- Sliding: 5', 6', 8', 12' wide
- French: 5', 6' wide (pair of doors)

### Measuring for a New Door

**For Replacement Doors:**
1. Measure width at top, middle, and bottom (use smallest measurement)
2. Measure height on left, center, and right (use smallest measurement)
3. Measure door thickness (usually 1-3/8" or 1-3/4")

**For New Openings:**
- Add 2" to door width for frame (rough opening)
- Add 2-1/2" to door height for frame and clearance
- Ensure adequate wall space for door swing

### Pre-Hung vs. Slab Doors

**Pre-Hung Doors:**
- Door already mounted in frame with hinges
- Easier and faster to install
- Best for new construction or full replacement
- More expensive but saves labor time

**Slab Doors:**
- Door only, no frame or hardware
- Requires existing frame in good condition
- More affordable
- Installation requires more skill

## Section 7: Installation Considerations

### Professional Installation vs. DIY

**When to Hire a Professional:**
- Entry door replacement (critical for security and weatherproofing)
- Patio door installation (heavy, requires precise leveling)
- New door openings (requires framing, drywall, finishing)
- Structural modifications

**DIY-Friendly Projects:**
- Replacing interior slab doors (if frame is in good shape)
- Installing new door hardware
- Painting or staining doors
- Installing storm doors

### Installation Checklist

**Before Installation:**
- ☐ Measure opening accurately
- ☐ Order correct door size and style
- ☐ Purchase all necessary hardware
- ☐ Ensure frame is square and level
- ☐ Check for rot or damage in frame

**During Installation:**
- ☐ Use shims to ensure door is level and plumb
- ☐ Secure frame to studs with appropriate fasteners
- ☐ Install weatherstripping for exterior doors
- ☐ Test door operation (smooth swing, proper latch)
- ☐ Seal gaps with insulation and caulk

**After Installation:**
- ☐ Install trim and casing
- ☐ Paint or stain as needed
- ☐ Install hardware and locks
- ☐ Test all functions (lock, deadbolt, smooth operation)

## Section 8: Maintenance & Care

### Entry Door Maintenance

**Every 6 Months:**
- Clean door surface with mild soap and water
- Inspect weatherstripping and replace if worn
- Lubricate hinges and locks
- Check for gaps or air leaks

**Annually:**
- Inspect door for damage (cracks, dents, rot)
- Touch up paint or stain as needed
- Tighten loose hardware
- Check and adjust door closer (if applicable)

**Wood Doors:**
- Re-stain or re-paint every 2-3 years
- Seal exposed wood to prevent moisture damage
- Sand and refinish if surface becomes worn

### Interior Door Maintenance

**As Needed:**
- Tighten loose hinges or hardware
- Adjust door if it sticks or doesn't latch properly
- Clean with damp cloth (avoid harsh chemicals)
- Touch up paint or finish scratches

### Patio Door Maintenance

**Monthly:**
- Clean door tracks and remove debris
- Wipe down glass with glass cleaner

**Every 6 Months:**
- Lubricate tracks and rollers
- Inspect weatherstripping and seals
- Check locks and adjust if needed

## Section 9: Security Features

### Entry Door Security Enhancements

**Solid Core or Solid Wood:**
- Hollow-core doors are easy to kick in
- Solid doors provide better resistance

**Deadbolt Locks:**
- Grade 1 deadbolts (highest security)
- 1-inch throw length minimum
- Hardened steel bolt

**Reinforced Strike Plates:**
- 3-inch screws into door frame studs
- Metal plate distributes force
- Prevents door from being kicked in

**Smart Locks & Video Doorbells:**
- Remote monitoring and control
- Activity logs and alerts
- Integration with home security systems

**Peepholes or Door Viewers:**
- Wide-angle view (180 degrees)
- See who's at the door before opening

**Security Hinges:**
- Non-removable pins
- Prevents door from being lifted off hinges

## Pro Tips from Hero Handyman Pro

**Invest in Your Entry Door:** Your front door is the first impression of your home. A quality entry door improves curb appeal, security, and energy efficiency—often returning 70-90% of its cost at resale.

**Don't Forget Weatherstripping:** Even the best door won't perform well without proper weatherstripping. This simple addition prevents drafts, reduces energy costs, and keeps out moisture and pests.

**Match Hardware Finishes:** For a cohesive look, use the same hardware finish throughout your home (or at least on the same floor). This small detail makes a big difference in overall design.

**Measure Twice, Order Once:** Door returns are expensive and time-consuming. Always double-check measurements before ordering, especially for pre-hung doors.

**Consider Future Needs:** If you plan to age in place, choose lever-style handles (easier to operate) and ensure doorways are at least 32" wide for wheelchair accessibility.
